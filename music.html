Essential Music Albums

Radiohead - Kid A

Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kid_A
While far from the most experimental piece of musical artistry that has ever been released, Radiohead's cult-classic Kid A is just experimental enough to be quite a trip and a sonic journey that you won't regret embarking on. There are many interpretations of the songs and even the overarching story presented, but that's a part of the fun: deciding for yourself what this bleak, electronic, absolutely mindbending project means to you.

Kendrick Lamar - Good Kid M.A.A.D. City

Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_Kid%2C_M.A.A.D_City
With a cover that proclaims itself a "short film", Kendrick Lamar's Good Kid, M.A.A.D. City establishes an immediate expectation of a major focus on storytelling. Of course, telling a story through song, or even a concept album with an overarching narrative is far from a brand new concept, but Kendrick knows and embraces this fact. Just because a piece of media uses common tropes or cliche does not mean it is inherently of lower quality, and this album pulls off one the oldest cliches known to the music industry so extremely well, that it seems like the freshest idea of the century. The best stories have more value the more they are experienced, and Kendrick Lamar's sophomore album is among the higher echelons of this priveleged group despite the relatively unconventional way it is told.

Hayley Williams - Petals for Armor

Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petals_for_Armor
Paramore frontwoman Hayley Williams has been an icon within the popular rock music scene for well over a decade now after the breakout hit of the group's sophomore album Riot! in 2007 with chart-topping hits like "Misery Business" and "crushcrushcrush". Unfortunately, not all that long ago, the band went through a period where they were seemingly bleeding members. The loss of guitarist Josh Farro, drummer Zac Farro, and bassist Jeremy Davis all happened prior to the year 2015. While Zac has since returned to the group, the other two members have not. During this strange time of membership limbo, even Hayley quit the band. Hayley has since returned to Paramore alongside Zac Farro on drums and new guitarist Taylor York, however she has most definitely experimented with the concept of a solo venture, likely in part due to her time away from the group. The first full-length solo album she released was 2020's Petals for Armor, a compilation of three "parts" that were initially released individually as EPs. A far cry from Paramore's early roots in pop-punk, pop-emo, and alternative rock, and still distinct from 2018's 80s dance-pop hit After Laughter; Petals is a deeply personal web of songs regarding abuse, trauma, and learning to heal all behind the juxtaposition of upbeat electronic instrumentals that accompany expressive vocal performances. These range from the potrayal of rage in title track and lead single "Simmer", to pained grief in "Sudden Desire", to infectious joy in "Taken" and "Pure Love". My personal favorite song from this LP, "Watch Me While I Bloom" is just as emotive as any of the formerly mentioned tracks while being accompanied by an insanely danceable bass-guitar groove. Despite its dark subject matter, Petals is an exceptionally fun listen that makes way for Hayley's unique voice as an artist that isn't always able to shine on Paramore's discography. In my eyes, this makes it a must listen.

System of a Down - Toxicity

Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity_(album)
During the 90s and early 2000s, mainstream metal was having a bit of an identity crisis. In the same time period as legendary releases like Death's Symbolic, Megadeth's Rust in Peace, and Rage Against the Machine's self-titled debut, rather forgettable rap/metal fusion projects were dominating rock radio airwaves. This movement would go on to be called "Nu-Metal", and while a select few bands that played in this style such as Korn and Slipknot would create long term relevancy for one of the worst musical fads of all time through the release of genuinely compelling music, many, many more simply faded into obscurity. Borrowing the guitar tones and riff styles of their rapping, screaming contemporaries while toting a noticable classic thrash inspiration and the integration of ideas from Armenian folk music, System of a Down burst into the scene in 1998 to massive sucess. Following the release of their rough around the edges but highly energetic self-titled debut, they dropped one of the most acclaimed and politically aware metal projects ever, 2001's Toxicity. From mega-hit "Chop-Suey" to deep cuts like "Deer Dance" and "Forest", Toxicity manages to be a timeless listen that seemingly only gets more relevant, rather than less so as it has aged.
